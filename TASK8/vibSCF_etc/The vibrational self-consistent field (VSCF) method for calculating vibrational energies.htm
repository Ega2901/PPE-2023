The vibrational self-consistent field (VSCF) method for calculating vibrational energies

Nicholas J. Wright, Galina M. Chaban, Joon O. Jung and R. Benny Gerber

To calculate the vibrational spectrum of polyatomic molecules it is best to solve the nuclear motion Schrödinger equation exactly. This, however, is currently possible only for relatively small systems (4-6 atoms). For larger systems, to make the problem computationally tractable, approximations must be introduced. Such an approximation must reduce the numerical effort involved as well as retaining the important dynamical features of the problem. One of the most commonly used methods is based upon the vibrational self-consistent field (VSCF) approximation [1, 2].

The computational difficulty in solving VSCF equations is in evaluation of Eq. (), the effective VSCF potential, which requires the evaluation of multidimensional integrals. Several approaches have been used to avoid this bottleneck, based upon expanding the potential function in powers of the normal modes [3] or only including interactions between pairs of normal modes in the potential expansion [3]. Monte Carlo integration of the multidimensional integrals was also proposed [3], but has not yet been applied to realistic molecular systems.

The VSCF method has been used for a variety of different polyatomics, all of which exploit its computational efficiency when applied to large systems. Examples include (Ar) [4], small water clusters (H O) [3] (recently up to n =8), peptide-water complexes [5] and glucose\ [6]. It has also been used for perhaps the largest system ever to have its vibrational spectrum calculated, the protein BPTI [7, 8]. This calculation included almost 200 hydration water molecules, giving a total of over 3500 interacting modes and showed that even for such a large system the spectrum remained assignable.

For systems where an analytical potential form is available, VSCF calculations are feasible even for systems having hundreds or thousands of coupled anharmonic modes. If such an analytical form is not available, a new potential function must be constructed. This approach, which requires a new potential to be constructed for each type of system studied, can become tedious and places as much emphasis on the construction of the potential as on the solution of the dynamical problem. These considerations led to the development of a new method, ab initio VSCF [9]. If Eqs.\ (-) are solved using grid-based methods\ [3], then the value of the potential energy function is only required at these discrete points. Therefore by interfacing an electronic structure code to the VSCF one, to compute the potential, the need for an analytical form for the potential is eliminated. This method opens the way for first-principles calculations of the vibrational spectroscopy of polyatomic molecules and, by comparing the results with experiment, provides a method for the calibration of the various electronic structure methods available today. Ab initio VSCF has so far been applied to several systems, (H O) , Cl (H O) (n=2-3), H O , H O and glycine\ [9, 10], with promising results.

The VSCF method has proved itself to be a powerful tool for the interpretation and prediction of vibrational spectroscopy. The increasing amounts of computer power becoming available should make it feasible to perform VSCF calculations on even larger systems with even greater accuracy.
